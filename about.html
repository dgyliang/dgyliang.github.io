My Story


Hi, I am David Liang. I was born in GuangZhou China on October 7th, 1999, and currently live in California, studying at UCSD with a major in Psychology.

I was born into this world into a loving family that includes two hard-working parents, and a very smart and funny older brother. However, before my developing mind was able to even fathom the existence of my father, he immigrated to the United States in Janurary 2000 in search of better opportunities. This left my mother alone to take care of both me and my brother. Eventually in 2008, My family was able to join my father in the States. Despite being in America for almost 10 years, my dad did not know how to speak English, and you've probably heard it all before - both of my parents ended up working low-paying laborous jobs in order to put food on the table. Being a child of strict Chinese parents, I also felt the same kind of pressure my parents felt. I was constantly reminded of how inadequate I am at school, being bullied almost everyday. I was also reminded of the financial hardships of the family every night at the dinner table, with my dad constantly scolding me to get good grades and get into a good college so we wouldn't have to live in my aunt's garage anymore. It was thanks to all those hardships that I was able to overcome adversity and become who I am today.

Food and Cooking


In China, there is one homogenous culture – that of the Han people. Everyone practically eats the same thing, regardless of the province one hails from. Rice, noodles and stir-fry are the staples of our diet. When I emigrated to this country, I finally discovered what it meant to be in a so-called “melting pot.” America is a mixture of different cultures, customs and ethnicities. People from even the farthest flung reaches of this earth flock here in search of new opportunities in hopes of achieving the “American dream.” This caused a boon for the variety of food on offer. Think hole-in-the-wall Mediterranean foods like kebabs, or nondescript Indian grocers selling the most exotic of spices sourced straight from the Asian subcontinent. And let’s not even get started on the seemingly endless varieties of dim sum at any given local Chinatown establishment. The very nature of this environment has allowed me to explore and develop a new hobby – cooking. In my spare time, I have taken cooking classes online as an expression of creativity. I love handcrafting the rice that goes into a good piece of nigiri sushi, and I also love the thrill of walking along the many isles of fresh produce in a farmers market. Even the simple act of sharing food amongst close relatives is exciting to me. To the Japanese, the preparation of food can be summed up as an art form. Likewise, the French emphasis on fresh cooking takes you back to a bygone era when traditional open-air markets reigned king. For Asian cultures like mine, there is no a la carte. Food is typically served in a communal setting and shared among family members. The dishes are divvied up as chopsticks clatter. I absolutely love observing how different cultures prepare their food and I always try to learn their style, which is why I love cooking.
